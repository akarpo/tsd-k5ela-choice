# Quantitative Analysis: Troy SD vs Peer + Outperformer Districts

**Universe:** 28 districts + 7 state averages, G3-G5 ELA, 2019-2025. Master dataset: `master_dataset.csv` (2,544 rows).

**Tests are NOT cross-state comparable in absolute terms** — within-district trends and state-relative gaps are valid; raw % are not. Different state thresholds: M-STEP "Proficient+Advanced" (MI), CAASPP "Met or Exceeded" (CA), STAAR "Meets Grade Level or Above" (TX), LEAP "Mastery+" (LA), MAAP "Proficient+Advanced" (MS), TCAP "On Track+Mastered" (TN), SBAC "Met Standard" (WA, DE), OST "Proficient" (OH).

## Executive findings

The expanded quantitative analysis sharpens — and in places amplifies — the original finding that Troy's curriculum is the most parsimonious explanation for its declining trend.

### Five headlines

1. **Troy at Grade 4 drops to 66.3% — BELOW Birmingham (75.4%), Bloomfield Hills (70.6%), Novi (68.5%), and Rochester (67.9%).** Troy has 37% Asian enrollment; Birmingham has 4%. At G3, Troy leads MI peers by ~1 point; by G4 it has fallen behind by 4–9 points. The G3→G4 collapse cannot be explained by demographics — it is most consistent with curriculum effects compounding through the "reading-to-learn" transition.

2. **Troy's state-relative G3 shift is essentially flat (+0.1pp).** MI state dropped from 45.1% to 38.9%; Troy dropped from 74.0% to 67.9%. Troy moved with the state, not against it — i.e. did not pull ahead, despite the state declining and Troy's demographics improving. Affluent peers like Birmingham (+27.7pp above state in 2025) and Bloomfield Hills (+26.7pp) are at similar or higher state-relative position from a lower-data baseline.

3. **SoR-aligned districts gained ground on their states; BL districts lost ground.**
   - **SoR group: mean +1.53pp state-relative shift** (9 districts, range -3.0 to +6.8). Best: Palo Alto +6.8, Sanger +6.0.
   - **Mixed group: +1.33pp** (Milpitas, Walnut Valley, Dublin).
   - **BL group: -0.72pp** (Troy, Plano -6.0, Coppell +1.0, Frisco +2.0).
   - **BL-drift (Long Beach): -3.0pp.**
   - The pattern is consistent: structured/explicit cores held or gained; balanced-literacy cores held barely or lost ground.

4. **Long Beach is empirically validated as the natural experiment.** Broad Prize 2003 under Open Court (Cohn era); shifted to TC Units of Study (Calkins) in 2010s; G3 ELA fell 55.6% → 48.3% (2019→2025), a -7.4pp drop **identical in magnitude to Troy's -6.1**. State-relative position fell -3.0pp. Same district, same leadership culture, same demographics — only the curriculum changed. This is the closest thing to a controlled trial against balanced literacy in the U.S. data.

5. **Aldine ISD's CKLA adoption (2020) shows the predicted G4-G5 cohort effect in 2025.** G3 +4pp 2019→2025 (no surprise — G3 cohort entered K only ~2 years post-adoption). **G4 +11pp 2024→2025. G5 +9pp 2024→2025.** This is precisely the lag pattern Bethlehem PA, Mississippi, and Tennessee all showed (3-year lag for first K cohort to reach G3, then progressive gains as cohorts move up). Broad-Prize winner that re-tooled into SoR rather than rest on laurels.

### The Asian subgroup story is decisive

Troy's Asian G3 cohort declined **-7.4 points** 2019→2025 — the worst Asian-subgroup trend of any peer with comparable demographics. The "ESL composition" explanation is empirically rejected by Texas BL districts with similar or larger Asian populations:

| District | Asian % | Asian G3 2019 | Asian G3 2025 | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Frisco ISD (TX, BL) | 42.5% | 79.0 | 84.0 | **+5.0** |
| Plano ISD (TX, BL) | 21.5% | 77.0 | 82.0 | **+5.0** |
| Coppell ISD (TX, BL) | ~25% | 80.0 | 87.0 | **+7.0** |
| Spring Branch ISD (TX) | mixed | 75.0 | 85.0 | **+10.0** |
| **Troy SD (MI, Calkins)** | **37%** | **80.3** | **72.9** | **−7.4** |

Troy is the only Asian-heavy comparable district to LOSE Asian-subgroup ground. The five Texas peers under balanced-literacy or similar approaches all GAINED. Whatever causes Troy's Asian decline, it is not Asian demographics.
