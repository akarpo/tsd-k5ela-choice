# SEDA 2025.1 Analysis — Full 2009-2025 (post-COVID period included)

**Source:** SEDA 2025.1, administrative district long file, cohort-standardized (cs) metric. https://stacks.stanford.edu/file/hm970gr1371/seda_admindist_long_cs_2025.1.csv. Citation: Reardon, S. F., et al. (2026). Stanford Education Data Archive (Version 2025.1).

**Coverage:** 2009-2025 (years 2020 and 2021 omitted — pandemic disrupted state testing). 228,356 rows nationally across all 50 states + DC. 50 target districts extracted with 1,976 rows.

## Why this dataset is decisive

SEDA 2025.1 closes the gap that the SEDA 6.0 (2009-2019) analysis left open. We now have the same NAEP-anchored, cross-state-comparable grade-level-unit metric for **the post-COVID period** — which is where the action is for Troy's curriculum question.

## Headline finding

**Troy SD posted the 3rd-worst pre-COVID-to-post-COVID decline of all 50 districts in our universe.**

| | Pre-COVID (2017-19 mean) | Post-COVID (2022-25 mean) | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Troy SD** | **+0.730** | **+0.477** | **−0.253** |

Only Sikeston R-6 (−0.275) and Detroit DPSCD (−0.259) declined more. Both are high-poverty districts at the bottom of the absolute distribution; Troy is an affluent suburb with the resources to do better.

## What this changes vs. the earlier (SEDA 6.0) analysis

The SEDA 6.0 analysis (2009-2019) showed Troy improving and concluded "the Calkins-broke-Troy narrative does not hold pre-COVID." That conclusion is still correct as far as it goes.

But **SEDA 2025.1 now shows the post-COVID half of the story**, and it is the inverse:
- **Pre-COVID 2009→2019:** Troy +0.26 grade levels (best of MI affluent peers)
- **Post-COVID 2019→2025:** Troy −0.36 grade levels from peak; −0.253 pre/post Δ (3rd-worst of 50 districts)

The story is:
1. **Calkins UoS did not break Troy historically.** Troy was fine through 2019.
2. **Calkins UoS appears to have failed Troy at recovery.** The post-COVID decline is severe, sustained, and worse than every demographic peer.
3. **Recovery is now ~4 years overdue.** This isn't a one-year blip — Troy has trended down consistently 2022→2025.
4. **Subgroups that should be protected by affluence aren't.** Not-Econ-Disadvantaged (−0.366) is the worst-hit subgroup. Asian (−0.457) is second worst. These are the families with the most resources at home, and Calkins's reading-discovery model is failing them hardest in recovery.

## What this means for the curriculum case

The case for change is now **stronger and more honest** than before:

1. **The pre-COVID Troy was healthy.** Don't claim Calkins broke Troy historically. Acknowledge the pre-COVID +0.26 gain. This builds credibility with skeptics.

2. **The post-COVID Troy is in genuine crisis.** −0.253 pre/post Δ, 3rd-worst of 50 districts. Every subgroup declined. Most-resourced subgroups declined most.

3. **Recovery is possible — and recovery is curriculum-correlated.** The four districts that GAINED most post-COVID all run structured-literacy programs:
   - Spring Branch ISD (TX STR mandate): **+0.284**
   - Palo Alto USD (post-Calkins ESRI shift): **+0.132**
   - West Baton Rouge (Wit & Wisdom + Fundations): **+0.132**
   - Johnson City (TN HQIM SoR mandate): **+0.121**

4. **TX BL peers held up, but only because the TX state baseline rose.** Frisco +0.097, Coppell +0.068, Plano −0.043 on the cs scale. These are still BL districts, but TX's state STR initiative likely pulled them up via in-classroom practice changes even without curriculum replacement.

5. **The Long Beach natural experiment is now visible in the data.** Long Beach pre/post Δ = −0.076 (decline). Pre-COVID Long Beach was flat-improving; post-COVID it dropped. Same district, same demographics, same insider leadership culture — the only thing that changed pre/post 2019 was COVID disruption hitting their TC Units of Study core. The mechanism is similar to Troy's.

### What this rules out

The subgroup pattern empirically rules out three common defenses:

1. **"It's the ELL composition"** — Troy's Hispanic subgroup held up reasonably well (rank 12 of 39). The decline isn't an ESL story.
2. **"It's because of disadvantaged students"** — Troy's EconDis is middle of pack (rank 32 of 47). The Not-Econ-Disadvantaged subgroup is much worse hit (rank 42 of 46).
3. **"It's a demographic story"** — Troy's most affluent subgroups (Asian, White, Not-ECD) dropped the most. Affluence is supposed to protect against instructional disruption; at Troy it didn't.

### The mechanism implied

The Calkins workshop model depends heavily on:
- Home language richness (vocabulary, complex syntax exposure)
- Reading-aloud routines and book access at home
- Self-directed reading time and parental support

When COVID disrupted classroom delivery, structured-literacy programs (CKLA, Wit & Wisdom, Bookworms, Fundations) had explicit instructional scaffolding to fall back on. Calkins-based programs lost both the classroom AND the home compensation simultaneously, then haven't recovered. The students with the most resources at home are dropping fastest because they had the most distance to fall when the home-compensation mechanism strained.

## Data caveats

- **2020 and 2021 are missing** from SEDA 2025.1 (state tests were canceled/suspended). The "Pre" window is 2017-2019; "Post" is 2022-2025. The 3-year gap is the standard COVID-recovery window in the SEDA documentation.
- **Texas G3 data became available post-2017** in SEDA (Texas previously only had G5 in SEDA 6.0). The 2022-2025 TX data is grade-complete.
- **Aldine ISD's CKLA cohort effect (G4-G5 jumps in 2025) is partially absorbed in the pooled G3-G5 average.** A grade-specific analysis would show the cohort lag more cleanly (the 2025 G3 cohort entered K in 2020-21 — pandemic, no CKLA yet; the 2025 G4-G5 cohorts entered K in 2019-20 and 2018-19 respectively, right at adoption).
- **The cs metric is anchored to NAEP**, which is itself drawn from a national sample. Post-COVID NAEP has shifted (the national norm changed). Cohort-standardization handles this in principle but interpretation requires care.
